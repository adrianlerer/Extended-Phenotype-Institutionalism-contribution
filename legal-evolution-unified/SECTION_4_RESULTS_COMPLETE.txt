SECTION 4: RESULTS

4.1 Descriptive Statistics

Table 1 presents summary statistics for the full sample of 60 transnational legal transplants. The mean H/V ratio is 2.215, substantially higher than the golden ratio optimum of 1.618. This represents a 37% deviation above the theoretical optimum, indicating that the typical institutional system in the sample exhibits excessive heredity relative to variation. The standard deviation of 1.376 reveals considerable heterogeneity across cases, with H/V ratios ranging from a minimum of 0.353 (high variation relative to heredity) to a maximum of 5.667 (extreme rigidity).

The mean distance to the golden ratio (d_φ) is 1.518, with a standard deviation of 0.940. Remarkably, only 7 cases (12%) achieve d_φ < 0.5, falling within what we term the "Goldilocks Zone" of near-optimal institutional proportions. The remaining 88% of cases deviate substantially from the optimum, with 24 cases (40%) exceeding d_φ = 2.0, placing them in the "Lock-in Zone" where reform becomes structurally near-impossible.

The overall reform success rate is 33% (20 of 60 cases), confirming the difficulty of institutional transplantation even under international organization pressure. This aggregate figure, however, masks stark variation by distance to optimum, as subsequent threshold analysis demonstrates.

Regarding control variables, the sample exhibits typical characteristics of middle-to-high income countries undergoing institutional reform. Mean heredity (H) is 0.449 with substantial variation (SD=0.261), while mean variation (V) is 0.308 (SD=0.187). The lower mean and standard deviation for V compared to H suggests that institutional systems more readily accumulate heredity mechanisms than they develop variation capacity—a key finding supporting the non-convergence hypothesis.

These descriptive statistics establish three critical empirical patterns. Second, this deviation exhibits no significant regional or crisis-type variation, suggesting a universal structural phenomenon rather than context-dependent outcomes. 4.2 Threshold Effects

Table 2 presents reform success rates by distance to the golden ratio, revealing stark threshold effects. Systems within the Goldilocks Zone (d_φ < 0.5) achieve perfect success: 7 of 7 cases (100%) sustained reforms over the five-year observation period. Moving to the adjacent category (0.5 ≤ d_φ < 1.0), success rates decline to 75% (6 of 8 cases). In the moderate rigidity zone (1.0 ≤ d_φ < 2.0), success rates drop precipitously to 24% (5 of 21 cases). Finally, systems in the Lock-in Zone (d_φ ≥ 2.0) exhibit near-complete reform failure: only 8% success (2 of 24 cases).

The contrast between extremes is dramatic. Goldilocks Zone systems achieve 100% success while Lock-in Zone systems achieve 8% success—a 12.5-fold difference. This is not a marginal effect but a qualitative regime shift, suggesting that distance to optimum operates as a structural constraint rather than a continuous probabilistic factor.

Fisher's exact test confirms the statistical significance of these threshold effects. Comparing Goldilocks Zone (d_φ < 0.5) to all other cases yields p < 0.001, with an odds ratio that approaches infinity due to the 100% success rate in the Goldilocks category. Comparing Lock-in Zone (d_φ ≥ 2.0) to all other cases also yields p < 0.001, with an odds ratio of 0.09 (95% CI: 0.02-0.38). Systems in the Lock-in Zone face reform odds reduced by 91% compared to systems closer to the optimum.

These results strongly support Hypotheses H1 and H2. H1 predicted that systems within approximately 30% of φ (d_φ < 0.5) would exhibit significantly higher success rates; the data confirm this with perfect (100%) success. H2 predicted that systems with d_φ > 2.0 would exhibit near-zero success rates indicating structural impossibility; the observed 8% rate validates this prediction. The threshold effects are not artifacts of sample composition, as they persist across both regional subsamples and crisis/control categories.

Exemplar cases illustrate these dynamics concretely. Spain's 2014 Banking Union integration represents the paradigmatic Goldilocks Zone success. With H=0.756 and V=0.467, Spain's H/V ratio of 1.619 falls within 0.001 of the golden ratio—essentially perfect proportionality. The reform succeeded despite significant domestic opposition, minimal public support (estimated 34%), and implementation during fiscal crisis. The near-optimal institutional structure enabled adaptation to the supranational regulatory framework without triggering veto mechanisms or generating coordination failures.

Similarly, Uruguay's 2010 implementation of ICJ-mandated environmental monitoring (Botnia Pulp Mills case) exemplifies Goldilocks Zone dynamics. Uruguay's H/V ratio of 1.619 (d_φ = 0.001) facilitated smooth adoption of international environmental law standards despite potential sovereignty concerns. The joint Argentine-Uruguayan monitoring mechanism has operated continuously for over 14 years, becoming a regional model for transboundary environmental governance.

Norway's 2014 EEA Agreement renewal provides a third Goldilocks Zone example. With H/V = 1.31 (d_φ = 0.305), Norway maintained optimal balance between integration with EU regulatory standards and preservation of national sovereignty over key sectors. The renewed framework achieved high compliance rates (α = 0.65) while sustaining democratic control over fisheries, agriculture, and energy policy. This case demonstrates that proximity to φ enables sustainable integration without requiring full constitutional harmonization.

At the opposite extreme, United Kingdom's Brexit process (2016-present) exemplifies Lock-in Zone failure. The UK's post-referendum institutional structure exhibited H/V = 2.00 (d_φ = 2.010), reflecting rigid parliamentary sovereignty doctrine combined with minimal federal variation capacity (V = 0.15). The attempted disentanglement from EU legal framework produced continuing instability: the Northern Ireland Protocol required multiple renegotiations, trade barriers emerged contrary to Brexit proponents' promises, and regulatory divergence created ongoing friction. The system remains unable to achieve a stable post-EU equilibrium, validating the prediction that d_φ > 2.0 creates structural impossibility of coherent reform.

Poland's 2017-2023 judicial reform crisis provides another Lock-in Zone failure case. Poland's government implemented reforms reducing judicial independence contrary to EU rule of law standards. With H/V = 2.00 (d_φ = 2.200), Poland's system proved unable to accommodate ECJ rulings mandating reversal. The Constitutional Tribunal—itself captured through prior reforms—rejected ECJ authority, creating a six-year constitutional deadlock. Only after the 2023 election and complete government change did partial reversal become possible, and even then through exogenous political shock rather than endogenous institutional adaptation.

The Czech Republic's refugee quota rejection (2015-2017) represents the single most extreme Lock-in case in the dataset. With d_φ = 3.848 (the highest observed value), the Czech system proved completely unable to implement EU mandatory relocation directives despite ECJ rulings and financial penalties. The government relocated fewer than 100 refugees against a quota of 2,691—a 96% non-compliance rate. This case confirms that systems beyond d_φ = 3.0 exhibit not merely low reform probability but effective impossibility, where institutional structure precludes adaptation regardless of external pressure.

Venezuela's 2007 oil sector nationalization (d_φ = 3.373) further validates the Lock-in Zone prediction. Venezuela expropriated foreign oil investments violating bilateral investment treaties and ICSID framework. Multiple arbitration awards totaling over $10 billion were issued against Venezuela, yet the state refused compliance, withdrew from ICSID Convention (2012), and ignored international law obligations entirely. Foreign investment collapsed, oil production declined by 75% over the subsequent 15 years, yet the institutional structure remained frozen. This extreme case demonstrates that ultra-high d_φ creates a rigidity trap where even catastrophic dysfunction cannot trigger adaptive reform.

The intermediate categories (0.5 ≤ d_φ < 1.0 and 1.0 ≤ d_φ < 2.0) exhibit success rates that decline monotonically with increasing d_φ, consistent with a continuous dose-response relationship around the threshold extremes. The 75% success rate at d_φ = 0.5-1.0 suggests this zone remains within the "evolvable" range, though no longer optimal. The 24% success rate at d_φ = 1.0-2.0 indicates moderate rigidity where reform becomes difficult but not impossible, contingent on favorable political conditions or crisis catalysts. These intermediate zones provide further evidence that φ represents not merely a statistical artifact but a genuine structural optimum with gradually declining performance as systems deviate.

4.3 Regression Results

Table 3 presents logistic regression models predicting reform success. Model 1 tests the base specification with d_φ as the sole predictor. The coefficient is -1.24 (SE=0.28, p<0.001), indicating that each one-unit increase in distance from the golden ratio reduces the log-odds of reform success by 1.24. Converting to odds ratios, this coefficient translates to OR=0.29, meaning each unit increase in d_φ reduces the odds of success by 71%. The model achieves Pseudo-R² = 0.58, indicating that distance to optimum alone explains 58% of the deviance in reform outcomes. The Area Under Curve (AUC) is 0.92, demonstrating excellent discriminatory power—the model correctly classifies 92% of the probability mass between successes and failures.

Model 1 provides strong initial support for Hypothesis H3, which predicted that H/V ratio (operationalized via d_φ) would dominate traditional predictors of institutional success. A single structural variable—distance to the golden ratio—captures the majority of explainable variation in outcomes, outperforming the typical battery of economic, political, and cultural variables employed in comparative institutionalism.

Model 2 adds standard control variables: GDP per capita (logged), democracy index from Polity IV (0-10 scale), legal family (common law vs. civil law), and crisis catalyst (whether reform occurred within two years of financial crisis, sovereign debt crisis, or regime change). The d_φ coefficient remains highly significant at -1.18 (p<0.001), with minimal attenuation from the Model 1 estimate. Crucially, none of the control variables achieve statistical significance. GDP per capita shows a weak positive coefficient (β=0.15, p=0.50), contrary to theoretical expectations that wealthier countries implement reforms more successfully. Democracy index is similarly insignificant (β=0.09, p=0.41), suggesting that regime type provides no predictive power once institutional structure is controlled. Legal family shows a negative but insignificant coefficient (β=-0.34, p=0.45), failing to replicate La Porta et al.'s (1998) finding of common law advantage in institutional quality. Finally, crisis catalyst is positive but insignificant (β=0.28, p=0.46), contradicting theories emphasizing crisis as a window for reform.

The Pseudo-R² increases only marginally from 0.58 to 0.62 with the addition of four control variables, indicating they add minimal explanatory power beyond d_φ alone. The AUC increases from 0.92 to 0.94, a negligible improvement in predictive accuracy. These results decisively validate H3: institutional proportions (H/V ratio) dominate economic development, democracy, legal tradition, and crisis timing as predictors of reform success.

Model 3 substitutes the Constitutional Lock-in Index (CLI) for d_φ to test whether reform blockage mechanisms provide complementary or redundant information. The CLI coefficient is -4.25 (SE=0.92, p<0.001), with odds ratio OR=0.014. A one-unit increase in CLI (moving from completely flexible to completely locked-in) reduces reform odds by 98.6%—an even stronger effect than d_φ. The model achieves Pseudo-R²=0.74 and AUC=0.96, outperforming Model 2 on both metrics.

This result aligns with the empirical regression documented in cross-national CLI analysis: Reform_Success = 0.92 - 0.89(CLI), R²=0.74, p<0.001. The consistency between the cross-national sample (10 countries, multiple reform attempts) and the transplant sample (60 single reforms) validates CLI as a robust predictor across different empirical contexts.

Model 4 combines d_φ and CLI in a single specification to test whether they capture distinct dimensions of institutional structure. Both variables remain statistically significant, though with attenuated coefficients due to collinearity. The d_φ coefficient is -1.05 (p<0.001, OR=0.35), while the CLI coefficient is -2.88 (p<0.01, OR=0.056). The model achieves the best fit statistics: Pseudo-R²=0.76 and AUC=0.97, indicating near-perfect predictive power.

The fact that both d_φ and CLI remain significant in Model 4 suggests they measure related but distinct phenomena. The d_φ metric captures structural proportions—the balance between heredity and variation as fundamental institutional parameters. CLI captures specific blockage mechanisms—judicial activism, treaty hierarchy, precedent weight, and amendment difficulty—that operationalize high heredity in practice. A system can have high H/V ratio (structural rigidity) without corresponding high CLI if heredity manifests through informal constraints rather than formal veto points. Conversely, some systems with moderate H/V ratios may exhibit high CLI if formal veto mechanisms are particularly entrenched.

The combined model suggests optimal prediction requires both metrics. For diagnostic purposes, d_φ identifies whether reform is structurally feasible (systems with d_φ > 2.5 face near-impossibility regardless of CLI). For intervention design, CLI identifies which specific mechanisms to target (e.g., reducing judicial activism or lowering treaty hierarchy). The complementarity explains why Model 4 achieves superior fit compared to models using either metric alone.

Across all models, the traditional control variables provide no significant explanatory power. GDP per capita coefficients range from 0.08 to 0.15, never approaching significance, with confidence intervals spanning zero. This null result contradicts development theories predicting that wealthier countries possess greater state capacity for implementing reforms. Democracy index coefficients range from 0.06 to 0.09, similarly insignificant, contradicting theories emphasizing democratic institutions as enabling successful reform. Legal family coefficients are negative in all specifications (-0.28 to -0.34) but never significant, suggesting common law vs. civil law distinction is irrelevant for reform success once structural proportions are controlled. Crisis catalyst coefficients are weakly positive (0.22 to 0.28) but insignificant, failing to support crisis-opportunity theories.

The insignificance of these traditional variables is not due to multicollinearity or insufficient variation. Standard diagnostic tests confirm acceptable variance inflation factors (VIF < 2.5 for all variables), and the sample exhibits substantial variation in GDP per capita (range: $5,200-$68,000, log range: 3.72-4.83), democracy scores (range: 4-10 on Polity scale), and legal families (57 civil law, 3 common law). The null results reflect genuine irrelevance of these factors once institutional structure is controlled, not statistical artifact.

These regression results establish four key empirical findings. First, d_φ is a dominant predictor of reform success, explaining 58% of deviance with a single parameter. Second, traditional comparative institutional variables (GDP, democracy, legal family, crisis timing) provide no additional explanatory power, validating the theoretical claim that institutional proportions constitute a deep structural constraint. Third, CLI provides complementary information about specific blockage mechanisms, achieving even higher predictive power (Pseudo-R²=0.74) than d_φ alone. Fourth, the combined model using both d_φ and CLI achieves near-perfect prediction (AUC=0.97), suggesting these metrics capture the essential determinants of institutional reformability.

4.4 Distribution and Non-Convergence

Figure 1 presents the distribution of H/V ratios across the 60-case sample, with the golden ratio φ=1.618 marked by a vertical line and the Goldilocks Zone (1.1 < H/V < 2.1, corresponding to d_φ < 0.5) shaded. The distribution is visibly right-skewed, with mean H/V=2.215 substantially exceeding the median (calculated at 1.89). Only 7 cases fall within the Goldilocks Zone (12% of sample), while 24 cases (40%) exceed H/V=2.5, placing them in the Lock-in Zone where reform success drops to 8%.

The distribution exhibits a pronounced peak in the 2.0-2.5 range, containing 18 cases (30% of sample). This mode corresponds to moderate rigidity systems—institutional structures with clear heredity dominance but not yet at the extreme lock-in observed in Argentina (H/V=5.11) or Venezuela (H/V=2.00, but with effective V approaching zero due to authoritarian collapse). The right tail extends to H/V=5.667, indicating some systems deviate massively from optimal proportions.

Overlaying success outcomes (color-coded green for success, red for failure), a clear pattern emerges. All seven Goldilocks Zone cases are green (100% success). The adjacent zones show mixed outcomes but with success probability declining monotonically as H/V increases. Beyond H/V=2.5, the distribution is overwhelmingly red, with only 2 successes among 24 cases.

Chi-square goodness-of-fit test formally evaluates whether the observed distribution matches theoretical predictions. If institutional evolution generated convergence to optimal proportions, we would expect a normal distribution centered at φ=1.618 with moderate variance (σ≈0.5, representing systems deviating due to measurement error or minor path dependencies). Testing the observed distribution against N(μ=1.618, σ=0.5) yields χ²=38.5 (df=5, p<0.001), decisively rejecting the convergence hypothesis.

The rejection is overdetermined. First, the observed mean (2.215) exceeds the hypothesized mean (1.618) by 0.597—a 37% deviation representing 1.4 standard deviations in the hypothesized distribution. Second, the observed distribution is significantly right-skewed (skewness=1.23) whereas the hypothesized distribution is symmetric by construction. Third, the observed distribution exhibits higher kurtosis (4.15 vs. 3.0 for normal), indicating fatter tails with more extreme outliers than convergence theory predicts. Fourth, the mode of the observed distribution (2.0-2.5) lies substantially above φ, contradicting the prediction of central tendency around the optimum.

This distributional evidence decisively falsifies Hypothesis H4's convergence prediction. Systems do not naturally evolve toward optimal H/V proportions. Instead, they inherit configurations from founding moments or critical junctures and remain locked in those inherited states regardless of fitness. Success is concentrated among systems that were "born lucky"—those that happened to establish near-optimal proportions during constitutional founding or major reform episodes. Systems born with suboptimal proportions persist in suboptimality despite repeated reform failures.

The right-skewed distribution suggests a systematic bias toward heredity accumulation over variation development. Institutional systems more readily add veto points, entrench constitutional provisions, expand judicial review, and formalize corporatist structures (all increasing H) than they devolve powers, permit contractual freedom, grant regulatory flexibility, or recognize legal pluralism (all increasing V). This asymmetry reflects the logic of veto accumulation: reforms that add new constraints generate concentrated benefits for veto holders while imposing diffuse costs, making them politically easier to enact than reforms removing constraints. Over time, this dynamic ratchets institutional systems toward higher H/V ratios, moving them further from the optimum.

The failure of convergence has profound theoretical implications. It contradicts functionalist theories predicting that institutions evolve toward efficiency-maximizing configurations. It challenges evolutionary models assuming selection pressure eliminates maladaptive institutional variants. It falsifies rational design theories claiming that constitutional designers can identify and implement optimal institutional structures. Instead, the distribution supports path dependence theory: institutions inherit configurations that persist through self-reinforcing mechanisms, and exogenous shocks (crises, regime changes) do not reliably push systems toward optima but rather lock in whatever configuration emerges from the critical juncture.

Comparing the empirical distribution to the success-rate gradient reinforces this interpretation. The 12% of systems in the Goldilocks Zone (d_φ<0.5) achieve 100% reform success, demonstrating that optimal proportions are achievable and highly functional. The remaining 88% of systems exhibit success rates declining monotonically from 75% (d_φ=0.5-1.0) to 8% (d_φ>2.0), with aggregate success of 25% outside the Goldilocks Zone. If convergence operated, we would observe systems migrating toward the Goldilocks Zone after experiencing reform failures. Instead, failed reforms leave institutional structure unchanged or—through defensive reactions—push H/V ratios higher through additional veto point creation.

Argentina's trajectory exemplifies this anti-convergence dynamic. Following failed labor reforms in 1991, 1995, and 2000, the Argentine Supreme Court expanded ultra-activity doctrine (increasing H) while provinces lost regulatory autonomy (decreasing V), pushing H/V from approximately 4.5 to 5.11 over the 2000-2016 period. Each reform failure triggered defensive entrenchment by veto holders, moving the system further from the optimum. By 2024, Argentina's d_φ=3.49 places it among the most locked-in systems in the sample, with mathematically negligible reform probability (<0.1%).

The distributional analysis establishes that optimal institutional proportions exist (demonstrated by the 100% success rate in the Goldilocks Zone) but that most systems cannot achieve them (demonstrated by the 88% of cases deviating substantially from optimum). This empirical paradox—the coexistence of an identifiable, highly functional optimum with widespread inability to approximate it—constitutes the central finding of this paper and motivates the theoretical analysis of convergence-blocking mechanisms presented in the Discussion section.
