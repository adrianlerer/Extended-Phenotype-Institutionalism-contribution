## 6.5 Policy Recommendations for Orsi Government

Based on our theoretical framework and empirical evidence, we offer **five strategic recommendations**:

### Recommendation 1: Build Pre-emptive Coalition (2025-2026)

**Rationale:** Crisis windows are **stochastic** (cannot be predicted). Orsi should invest in coalition-building **before crisis** to be ready when window opens.

**Actions:**
- **Informal negotiations with PN leadership** (Delgado): Identify PN's policy priorities (infrastructure, interior development) that FA can offer as exchange
- **Parliamentary working group:** Create cross-party commission on pension sustainability (technocratic framing)
- **Consensus-building:** Secure agreement on **diagnosis** (BPS deficit unsustainable) before proposing **solution** (reform design)

**Timeline:** 2025-2026 (12-18 months negotiation)

**Precedent:** Brazil's Bolsonaro spent 8 months (Jan-Aug 2019) negotiating with Centr√£o before pension vote, ensuring 2/3 majority.

### Recommendation 2: Frame Reform as "BPS Strengthening," Not Cuts

**Rationale:** Memetic analysis (Section 2) shows **framing** determines success. "Defending BPS" has higher fitness than "pension cuts."

**Discourse strategy:**
- ‚ùå Avoid: "We must cut pensions to save fiscal sustainability" ‚Üí triggers loss aversion
- ‚úÖ Emphasize: "We strengthen BPS to guarantee pensions for our children" ‚Üí intergenerational fairness
- ‚úÖ Villain framing: "Previous governments (PN 2020-2025) neglected BPS, we fix it" ‚Üí blame externalization

**Communication plan:**
- **President Orsi speeches:** Personal testimony about family's dependence on BPS (humanize)
- **Testimonial ads:** Middle-class workers (age 30-40) saying "I want BPS to exist when I retire"
- **Social media:** Infographics showing "BPS in 2040 without reform" (fear appeal) vs "BPS with reform" (hope)

**Precedent:** Colombia's Petro (2023) framed pension reform as "expanding coverage to informal workers," not cuts, gaining median voter support.

### Recommendation 3: Design Progressive Reform (Rich Pay More)

**Rationale:** Uruguay's PR median voter (center-left) accepts adjustments if **perceived as fair** (progressive distribution).

**Policy design:**
- **Differentiated cuts by income quintile:**
  - Bottom 40%: Zero cuts (minimum pension +10%)
  - Middle 40%: Moderate cuts (replacement rate 78%‚Üí74%)
  - Top 20%: Larger cuts (replacement rate 78%‚Üí68%, cap high pensions)

- **Wealth tax financing:** 1% wealth tax on assets >USD 1M (raises ~0.5% GDP) earmarked for minimum pension increase

- **Transparency:** Publish individual-level impact calculator: "Enter your salary ‚Üí see your pension change"

**Median voter logic:** If **median voter perceives personal gain** (or zero loss) while wealthy bear costs, reform acceptable even if aggregate cuts.

**Precedent:** Brazil 2019 reform included **rural pension exemption** (protecting low-income base) while cutting public sector pensions (perceived as privileged).

### Recommendation 4: Exploit Crisis Window (If/When It Arrives)

**Rationale:** Our model (Table 4.11) shows crisis **doubles success probability** (27%‚Üí64%).

**Crisis indicators to monitor:**
- **Sovereign spread >300 bps** (currently 180 bps) ‚Üí bond market panic
- **GDP growth <‚àí1%** for 2 consecutive quarters ‚Üí recession
- **Regional contagion:** Brazil or Argentina crisis spilling over

**Action plan when crisis triggers:**
1. **Fast-track legislation** (submit bill within 30 days of crisis onset)
2. **Emergency framing:** "We have 90 days to avoid IMF program"
3. **Elite consensus:** Joint statement by FA + PN + PC + business + unions (even if symbolic)
4. **International validation:** Invite IMF/World Bank endorsement (technocratic authority)

**Timeline:** Crisis window is **short-lived** (3-6 months). Orsi must act decisively, no time for prolonged negotiation.

**Precedent:** Ecuador 1999‚Äîbanking crisis (GDP ‚àí5.3%) enabled President Mahuad to pass pension reform via legislative emergency powers, bypassing normal veto points.

### Recommendation 5: Alternative‚ÄîIncremental Stealth Reforms

**Rationale:** If grand coalition unattainable, pursue **low-salience incremental reforms** that cumulatively reduce deficit without triggering referendum.

**Stealth tactics:**
- **Administrative tightening:** Stricter disability pension requirements (saves 0.3% GDP, low visibility)
- **Indexation change:** Switch from wage-indexation to CPI-indexation (saves 0.5% GDP over 10 years, gradual)
- **Contribution evasion enforcement:** Audit high-income self-employed (saves 0.4% GDP, appears as "anti-rich")
- **Retirement age for new entrants only:** Workers under age 25 face age 65 (affects nobody currently, no mobilization)

**Cumulative savings:** 1.2-1.5% GDP over 10 years‚Äînot sufficient to solve crisis, but **buys time** until crisis window or coalition becomes feasible.

**Precedent:** Argentina 2016-2018‚ÄîMacri implemented stealth pension cuts via indexation formula change (Presidential decree), avoiding legislative/judicial veto until 2020 (when CSJN reversed).

## 6.7 Broader Implications: Uruguay as Test of Democratic Reform Capacity

Uruguay's 2025-2030 trajectory will test a fundamental question: **Can mature democracies reform welfare states without crisis?**

**Optimistic view (Acemoglu & Robinson 2012):** Inclusive institutions enable pre-emptive reform via deliberation and coalition-building. Uruguay's strong democratic quality (Freedom House 98/100) and low corruption suggest capacity for consensual adjustment.

**Pessimistic view (Tsebelis 2002, this paper):** **Veto player theory** predicts reform failure in proportional systems with multiple veto points (referendum, judicial review, legislative supermajorities). Even with ultraactivity eliminated, residual veto points block change until crisis forces elite cooperation.

**Our framework's prediction:** The pessimistic view prevails **absent crisis**. Uruguay's post-1991 experience (29 of 46 reforms succeeded, 63%) shows ultraactivity elimination **necessary but not sufficient**. The remaining 37% failures‚Äîconcentrated in high-salience domains (pensions, labor)‚Äîreflect **median voter constraints + referendum veto**.

**If Scenario 3B materializes** (crisis-triggered grand coalition), Uruguay would validate the **conditional optimism** thesis: democracies **can** reform, but require **four necessary conditions** simultaneously (no ultraactivity + no other high-toxicity vetoes + median voter compatibility + crisis timing). This demanding conjunction explains why reform success is **stochastic** rather than deterministic.
