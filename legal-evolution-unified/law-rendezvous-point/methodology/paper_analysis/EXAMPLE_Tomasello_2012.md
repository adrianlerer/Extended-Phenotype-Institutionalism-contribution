**Pass 1 Summary** (200 words):

Tomasello et al. propose that human cooperation evolved via **two key steps**, not gradual accumulation. ## üìñ PASS 2: GRASP CONTENT (1 hour 15 min)

### A. Argument Structure

**Main Thesis**: 
Human cooperation is uniquely characterized by **shared intentionality** (joint goals, mutual support), which evolved via two steps:
1. Obligate collaborative foraging ‚Üí Mutualistic cooperation + early shared intentionality
2. Cultural group selection ‚Üí Group-mindedness + social norms

**Supporting Arguments**:

**Arg 1: Standard models insufficient**  
Evidence: Kin selection, reciprocal altruism, indirect reciprocity all exist in non-human primates, yet they don't cooperate like humans. Therefore, something else must explain human uniqueness.

**Arg 2: Shared intentionality is the key difference**  
Evidence: 
- Experimental: Children 3yo help partners even when costly, chimps don't (Warneken et al. 2006)
- Comparative: Human infants coordinate attention ("joint attention") much earlier than chimps
- Cross-cultural: All human societies have collaborative activities (hunting, childcare)

**Arg 3: Step 1 = Collaborative foraging (~2M years ago)**  
Evidence:
- Archaeological: Homo erectus shows evidence of collaborative hunting (large game, tool use)
- Anatomical: Homo erectus body plan suited for endurance running (pursuit hunting)
- Cognitive: Obligate collaboration requires joint goals (can't succeed alone) ‚Üí Selection for shared intentionality

**Arg 4: Step 2 = Cultural group selection (~100k years ago)**  
Evidence:
- Archaeological: Modern human behavior appears ~100k years ago (symbolic artifacts, complex tools)
- Demographic: Population density increases ‚Üí Inter-group competition
- Cultural: Groups with stronger norms outcompete groups with weaker norms

**Arg 5: Empirical validation in children**  
Evidence:
- Hamann et al. (2011): Children 3yo share equitably ONLY when both contributed (not when windfall)
- Contrast with chimps: Chimps share based on dominance/harassment, not equity principle
- Conclusion: Human children have **second-personal morality** (fairness toward partners)

**Conclusion**: 
Human cooperation uniquely stems from **two evolutionary steps** that created motivations (not just capacities) for shared intentionality and group-mindedness. This explains why humans cooperate at scales impossible for other primates.

### B. Critical Evaluation

**Strengths**:
1. **Clear theoretical model**: Two-step structure is testable and falsifiable
2. **Cross-disciplinary evidence**: Archaeology + developmental psychology + primatology
3. **Addresses timing**: Proposes actual evolutionary timeline (not just "sometime in human evolution")
4. **Comparative method**: Systematically contrasts humans with great apes
5. **Empirical rigor**: Child experiments control for confounds (e.g., windfall vs earned)

**Weaknesses**:
1. **Archaeological evidence limited**: ~2M years ago collaborative foraging is plausible but not definitively proven
2. **Timeline speculative**: "~100k years ago" for Step 2 is rough estimate, could be earlier/later
3. **Mechanism underspecified**: HOW does obligate collaboration select for shared intentionality? (Genetic? Cultural? Co-evolutionary?)
4. **Alternative explanations underexplored**: Could language alone explain shared intentionality? (Chicken-egg problem)
5. **Group selection controversial**: Some evolutionary biologists skeptical of cultural group selection efficacy

**Gaps**:
1. **Missing data**: More archaeological evidence needed for collaborative foraging timeline
2. **Unexplored alternatives**: What about other selective pressures? (e.g., coordinated defense, cooperative breeding)
3. **Scope limitations**: Focuses on cooperation, doesn't extend to legal norms explicitly (but implications clear)

**Pass 2 Summary** (500 words):

Tomasello's two-step model is **compelling and well-supported**, but with caveats on timeline precision.

**Step 1 (Mutualism)**: The claim that obligate collaborative foraging (~2M years ago with Homo erectus) selected for shared intentionality is **theoretically sound** but **archaeologically under-constrained**. Evidence: (1) H. erectus anatomical adaptations (endurance running), (2) Large game remains at sites, (3) Tool use requiring coordination. However, WHEN exactly shared intentionality emerged is uncertain - could be H. erectus (~2M), H. heidelbergensis (~600k), or even early H. sapiens (~300k). This is CRITICAL for the Rubicon question.

**Step 2 (Group-mindedness)**: Cultural group selection (~100k years ago) is more speculative. Evidence: (1) Modern behavior appears ~100k years ago, (2) Population density increases, (3) Symbolic artifacts suggest group identity. But mechanisms are unclear - HOW does group competition select for norms? Henrich's work on punishment may fill this gap.

**Empirical Evidence**: The child experiments are **robust**. Key finding: Children 3yo share equitably ONLY when both contributed to obtaining resource (Hamann et al. 2011). This shows **partner-specific fairness**, not just general altruism. Contrast with chimps (who share based on harassment/dominance) is striking. This supports **shared intentionality** as human-unique.

**Strengths**: (1) Falsifiable predictions (test other primates for collaborative foraging), (2) Integrates archaeology + psychology + primatology, (3) Provides timeline (testable against fossil/archaeological record).

**Weaknesses**: (1) Timeline imprecise (~2M could be ¬±1M years), (2) Mechanism underspecified (genes? culture? both?), (3) Alternative explanations (language-first? cooperative breeding?) not fully explored.

**Relevance to Rubicon**: **CRITICAL**. If shared intentionality emerged ~2M years ago, this is the PRIMARY RUBICON for law. Why? Because shared intentionality enables:
- **Joint commitment** (proto-contracts: "we agree to X")
- **Partner-specific obligations** (foundation of reciprocity norms)
- **Third-party enforcement potential** (impartial observers can judge "we agreed to X but you violated")

Everything after (language, agriculture, writing, codes) is REFINEMENT, not qualitative leap. Language makes explicit what's already implicit (joint commitments). Writing stores what's already agreed (norms). Codes systematize what's already enforced (rules).

**Critical Question for Pass 3**: Can we narrow down the timeline? Is there archaeological/genetic evidence to distinguish H. erectus (~2M) vs H. heidelbergensis (~600k) vs H. sapiens (~300k) as the inflection point?

### A. Re-Implementation

**Can you reconstruct the two-step model?**
- [X] Yes, fully

**Reconstruction**:

**Step 0 (Baseline)**: Great ape ancestor (~6-7M years ago) has:
- Kin selection (Hamilton 1964)
- Reciprocal altruism (Trivers 1971)
- Food-sharing (Mitani & Watts 2001)
- But: Individual-level competition, no joint goals

**Step 1 (Mutualism, ~2M years ago)**:
- **Ecological change**: Climate variability ‚Üí Woodland/savanna mosaic
- **Niche shift**: Homo erectus adopts collaborative foraging (hunting large game)
- **Obligate collaboration**: Cannot succeed alone (prey too large/fast)
- **Selection pressure**: Individuals who can coordinate > Individuals who cannot
- **Evolutionary innovation**: **Shared intentionality** (joint goals, role differentiation, helping)
  - Joint attention (both watch prey together)
  - Joint commitment (both pursue until caught)
  - Role reversal (I drive, you ambush; next time reverse)
- **Outcome**: Humans develop unique motivation to help collaborative partners

**Step 2 (Group-mindedness, ~100k years ago)**:
- **Demographic change**: Population density increases
- **Inter-group competition**: Groups compete for resources/territory
- **Cultural group selection**: Groups with stronger norms > Groups with weaker norms
- **Selection pressure**: Individuals who conform > Individuals who deviate
- **Evolutionary innovation**: **Cultural norms** + **Punishment** + **Group identity**
  - Social norms (e.g., food-sharing rules, fairness)
  - Punishment of deviants (ostracism, reputation)
  - Group symbols (language dialects, rituals)
- **Outcome**: Humans develop loyalty to group, conformity to norms

**Key Insight**: Step 1 is COGNITIVE (shared intentionality), Step 2 is CULTURAL (norms). Both necessary for modern cooperation.

**Bottlenecks / Innovations**:
- **Step 1 bottleneck**: Shared intentionality requires **second-personal engagement** (not third-personal observation). This is qualitative leap, not quantitative.
- **Step 2 bottleneck**: Cultural norms require **punishment** (enforcement). Without punishment, norms collapse to lowest common denominator.

### B. Critical Deep Dive

**Implicit Assumptions Found**:
1. **Language co-evolved with shared intentionality** (not explicitly stated but implied)
   - Issue: Chicken-egg problem. Did language enable shared intentionality, or vice versa?
   - Tomasello's view (from other papers): Shared intentionality preceded language (gestures ‚Üí words)

2. **Collaborative foraging was THE key niche** (not just one of many)
   - Issue: What about cooperative breeding (Hrdy), coordinated defense, or pair bonding?
   - Evidence: Collaborative foraging is uniquely **obligate** (can't succeed alone)

3. **Group selection was strong enough** to overcome individual selection
   - Issue: Many evolutionary biologists skeptical (within-group competition opposes between-group selection)
   - Counter: Cultural group selection can be strong if migration is low and groups are small (Boyd & Richerson 2002)

**Errors/Weaknesses Identified**:

**1. Timeline Uncertainty (Moderate severity)**
- **Type**: Evidentiary
- **Description**: "~2M years ago" is based on indirect evidence (H. erectus anatomy, large game remains). Could be earlier (H. habilis ~2.5M?) or later (H. heidelbergensis ~600k?).
- **Severity**: [ ] Minor [X] Moderate [ ] Major
- **Impact**: Affects Rubicon pinpointing. If shared intentionality emerged 600k years ago (not 2M), Rubicon is 3x more recent.

**2. Mechanism Underspecified (Minor severity)**
- **Type**: Theoretical
- **Description**: HOW does obligate collaboration select for shared intentionality? Genetic mutations? Cultural learning? Gene-culture co-evolution?
- **Severity**: [X] Minor [ ] Moderate [ ] Major
- **Impact**: Model is descriptive (what evolved) not mechanistic (how it evolved). Not fatal, but limits predictive power.

**3. Alternative Hypotheses Not Fully Tested (Minor severity)**
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Description**: Could language-first explain data? (Language ‚Üí Shared intentionality, not vice versa)
- **Severity**: [X] Minor [ ] Moderate [ ] Major
- **Impact**: Tomasello has other papers arguing against language-first (gestures precede words in infants), but not fully addressed here.

**Alternative Explanations Considered**:

**H': Language evolved first, enabled shared intentionality**
- **Could data support it?**: [X] Yes, possible
- **How to test**: Look for language-like behavior in pre-shared-intentionality species. If language requires shared intentionality (joint attention to learn words), then shared intentionality must come first. ### C. Innovation Assessment

**Truly Novel**:
1. **Two-step model**: Previous models (Trivers, Axelrod) were one-step (reciprocity). Tomasello shows TWO qualitative leaps.
2. **Shared intentionality as uniqueness**: Shifts focus from capacities (intelligence, memory) to MOTIVATIONS (why cooperate, not just how).
3. **Partner-specific morality**: Children's equity norm applies ONLY to collaborative partners (not strangers). This is new empirical finding.

**Incremental**:
1. **Collaborative foraging hypothesis**: Not entirely new (Isaac 1978 proposed this), but Tomasello provides more precise evolutionary mechanism.

**Claims Not Fully Supported**:
