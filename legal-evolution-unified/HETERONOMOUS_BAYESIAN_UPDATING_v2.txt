INTRODUCTION A. The Puzzle of Irreversible Dysfunction On December 29, 2017, Argentine President Mauricio Macri signed Decree 27/2018, initiating what his administration characterized as the country's most comprehensive labor reform since the 1957 constitutional convention. The decree attempted to modify collective bargaining procedures and dismissal requirements embedded in Article 14bis of the National Constitutionâ€”a provision establishing workers' rights to "protection against arbitrary dismissal," "stable employment," and collective organization that Argentine courts had interpreted over six decades as mandating near-absolute job security with judicial pre-approval for dismissals. Within ninety-six hours of the decree's publication, labor tribunals in five provinces issued preliminary injunctions blocking implementation. Within three months, facing coordinated judicial resistance, extensive academic criticism, and unified opposition from the nation's 185 bar associations, the Macri administration quietly withdrew the reform. The constitutional provision, functionally unchanged since 1957, survived its latest challenge. This outcome was neither anomalous nor surprising. Since Article 14bis entered constitutional force in May 1957, Argentina has attempted major labor law reform under seven distinct governments representing dramatically different ideological orientations: the developmentalist Frondizi administration (1958-1962), the bureaucratic-authoritarian OnganÃ­a dictatorship (1966-1970), the military junta under Videla (1976-1983), the neoliberal Menem government with three separate attempts (1991, 1995, 2000), the center-right De la RÃºa coalition (2001), the market-oriented Macri presidency (2015-2019), and most recently the libertarian Milei administration (2023-present). All seven attempts failed.

The constitutional provision remains substantively unmodified for ninety-three years, despite Argentina experiencing labor costs 2.3 times the Latin American regional average, unemployment persistently above 7% since 1974, informal employment encompassing 47% of the workforce as of 2024, and GDP per capita growth rates substantially below regional comparators over the same period. The persistence becomes more puzzling when examined comparatively. Brazil, Chile, and Spainâ€”all possessing similar constitutional labor provisions adopted during roughly the same historical period and all facing comparable economic pressuresâ€”successfully reformed their labor regimes during the same timeframe that Argentina repeatedly failed. Brazil reformed its ConsolidaÃ§Ã£o das Leis do Trabalho (CLT) in 2017 after seventy-three years of stability, substantially modifying dismissal procedures, collective bargaining structures, and working time regulations. Chile reformed Articles 306 through 381 of its Labor Code six times between 1990 and 2023, each reform materially altering employment relationships. Spain reformed the constitutional framework governing Articles 35-38 through legislative interpretation and statutory modification five times between 1994 and 2022, including major restructurings during the 2008-2012 financial crisis. Yet Argentina, facing arguably more severe labor market dysfunction than any of these comparators, achieved zero successful reforms across the same period. Constitutional scholars typically explain persistent institutional resistance through three well-established theoretical frameworks. Veto player theory (Tsebelis 2002) identifies the number and ideological distance of actors capable of blocking change, predicting that reform becomes increasingly difficult as veto players multiply and polarize. Yet this framework struggles with the Argentina-Brazil comparison: both countries require congressional supermajorities for constitutional amendment (Argentina requires two-thirds approval; Brazil requires three-fifths approval in two separate votes plus presidential assent), yet Brazil succeeded while Argentina failed repeatedly. Path dependence theory (North 1990; Pierson 2000) describes how initial institutional choices constrain subsequent options through increasing returns, network effects, and coordination expectations. This approach effectively describes the phenomenonâ€”institutions persist despite dysfunctionâ€”but provides no mechanism explaining how past choices exercise such powerful constraints on present actors who clearly perceive and suffer from the dysfunctional consequences. Interest group capture theories (Olson 1965) identify organized labor as the blocking force, but face an empirical anomaly: Argentine union density (25% of formal sector workers) is substantially lower than in Sweden (67%), Denmark (67%), or Finland (64%), all of which reformed labor laws multiple times since 1990. If union power alone explained resistance, high-density countries should exhibit greater reform difficulty than Argentina, yet the opposite pattern appears.

What these established frameworks share is their treatment of institutions as equilibria or constraints rather than as systems actively defending founding principles through identifiable cognitive mechanisms operating across thousands of individual actors. Veto player theory models strategic interaction but abstracts from the psychological processes generating preferences. Path dependence describes cumulative causation but leaves the accumulation mechanism theoretically underspecified. Interest group theories identify blocking coalitions but cannot explain why certain coalitions succeed in some jurisdictions while failing in others with apparently greater structural advantages. None provides a mechanistic account explaining how institutions resist change at the cognitive levelâ€”how individual judges, professors, lawyers, and bureaucrats coordinate to defend constitutional provisions without explicit conspiracy, how professional socialization transmits resistance across generations, how precedent networks and doctrinal elaboration function as defense mechanisms, or why these mechanisms operate more powerfully in some jurisdictions than others. B. The Cognitive Turn: Dennett's Bayesian Foundation The missing foundation lies in cognitive science, specifically in Daniel Dennett's revolutionary reconceptualization of how minds generate normative perceptions. In From Bacteria to Bach and Back: The Evolution of Minds (2017), Dennett synthesizes decades of neuroscience and computational modeling to demonstrate that brains operate as Bayesian expectation machines implementing what he terms "hierarchical predictive coding." The architecture functions through continuous prediction generation: the brain creates probabilistic expectations about incoming sensory data, compares predictions with observations, and updates beliefs when predictions failâ€”but only when the prediction error exceeds certain thresholds determined by the prior's strength or "precision." Critically, Dennett demonstrates this Bayesian updating operates without requiring conscious reasoning or explicit representation of the statistical principles involved. He terms this phenomenon "competence without comprehension": organisms exhibit normatively sophisticated behaviorâ€”tracking social reciprocity, avoiding defectors, responding to reputation signalsâ€”without representing the reasons for that behavior or understanding the principles making it effective. Dennett's most profound insight for institutional analysis concerns the origin of seemingly objective normative properties. Certain perceptions of rightness, necessity, or fundamentality are not discoveries of objective features in the world but rather projections of Bayesian priors repeatedly confirmed by absence of contradiction. His canonical example merits extended quotation:

When these meta-expectations are consistently confirmedâ€”the baby reliably elicits protective urges, sugar reliably produces pleasure, Article 14bis violations reliably generate disapprovalâ€”our brains interpret this reliability as evidence of objective properties in the world rather than as stable patterns in our own prediction machinery. The sweetness seems to reside in the sugar, the cuteness in the baby, the fundamentality in the constitutional provision. All are Bayesian priors mistaken for objective discoveries. Dennett grounds this architecture in the emerging neuroscience of predictive coding, drawing heavily on work by Karl Friston, Andy Clark, and others demonstrating that cortical processing operates hierarchically: higher levels generate predictions about lower-level activations, lower levels signal prediction errors upward, and the system minimizes total prediction error (formally termed "free energy" in Friston's framework) through belief updating. As Dennett explains: "From this perspective, the brain's strategy is to continuously create forward models or probabilistic anticipations and use the incoming signals to prune and improve their accuracy, if need be. When the organism is on a roll, in familiar territory, the corrections that arrive are reduced to a trickle, and the brain's suppositions, unopposed, give it a good head start on what to do next" (2017, pp. 159-160). The architecture explains a surprising neuroanatomical fact: in visual pathways, descending connections (predictions flowing from higher to lower areas) substantially outnumber ascending connections (sensory signals flowing from retina to cortex). The brain spends more resources generating expectations than processing raw inputs, relying on sensory data primarily to correct predictions when they fail rather than to construct representations from scratch.

But Dennett explicitly restricts his analysis to individual organisms. He never extends the Bayesian framework to institutions composed of thousands of individuals maintaining collective priors through professional socialization, precedent networks, and doctrinal elaboration. He never formalizes how norms are learned through observing reactions to behaviors rather than through observing behavioral frequencies themselves. He provides no mechanism explaining why certain institutional priors resist updating despite abundant contradictory evidence. And he offers no quantitative methods for measuring prior strength or predicting when updating will occur versus when priors will be defended through world-changing actions rather than belief-changing revisions. This article makes those extensions. Building on Dennett's cognitive foundation, I develop three original theoretical contributions that connect individual Bayesian cognition to institutional lock-in, providing constitutional theory with mechanistic explanations grounded in cognitive architecture, computational neuroscience, and evolutionary biology. C. Three Theoretical Contributions CONTRIBUTION 1: Heteronomous Bayesian Updating Standard Bayesian learning assumes agents update priors based on observed frequencies in the environment. If you observe cooperation in 90% of public goods games, Bayesian updating predicts you will infer cooperation is the norm and increase your prior accordingly. If you observe hitting in 40% of childhood interactions, you should infer hitting is acceptably common and adjust expectations. But this frequency-based learning cannot explain the persistence of norms that contradict empirical observations. Why do children learn "hitting is wrong" despite observing hundreds of hitting instances among siblings, peers, and media depictions? Why do Argentine lawyers believe Article 14bis is fundamental despite observing ninety-three years of economic dysfunction attributable to the provision? Why do norms frequently persist when behavioral evidence suggests they are counterproductive? The resolution lies in recognizing that humans learn norms not primarily from observing what people do but from observing how others react to what people do. Children learn hitting is wrong not from the frequency of hitting behavior but from the pattern of reactions: parental horror and punishment, sibling complaints and retaliation, teacher sanctions and time-outs, narrative depictions of villains as aggressors. These reactions carry different information than behavioral frequencies. A single instance of authoritative disapproval (mother's shocked reaction to hitting) can outweigh dozens of observations of the behavior itself (siblings hitting each other daily). I formalize this as Heteronomous Bayesian Updating (HBU):

 CONTRIBUTION 2: Institutional Active Inference Karl Friston's free energy principle (2010) demonstrates that biological systems minimize prediction error through two complementary strategies: perceptual inference (updating internal models to match observations) and active inference (acting to make observations match internal models). Individual brains seamlessly integrate both strategiesâ€”you might update your belief about coffee's location if you look left and see it on the right, or you might reach right to move the cup to match your leftward expectation. Strategy selection depends on which minimizes prediction error more efficiently given current circumstances and action costs. I extend this framework to institutions by demonstrating that when priors are collectively held across thousands of actors whose professional identities, expertise, and institutional positions depend on prior stability, active inference becomes vastly cheaper than perceptual inference. Argentine labor courts facing reform proposals contradicting Article 14bis encounter large prediction errors: the reform implies P(14bis necessary) should decrease from approximately 0.99 to perhaps 0.30, a massive revision. The courts face two strategies for minimizing this prediction error. Perceptual inference would require updating the prior: revising sixty years of precedent, rewriting labor law curricula at all law schools, retraining

CONTRIBUTION 3: Extended Phenotype Integration Richard Dawkins' extended phenotype theory (1982) established that genes build artifacts extending beyond individual bodies to defend and propagate themselves: beaver dams protect offspring, spider webs capture resources, caddisfly cases provide mobile shelter. I demonstrate that legal institutions function as extended phenotypes of constitutional memes, actively defending founding priors through coordinated structures. Article 14bis generated four institutional structures that function as its extended phenotype: (1) 187 specialized labor tribunals created between 1958 and 1980, providing enforcement capacity; (2) 3,847 registered labor unions with in-house legal departments staffed by 14bis specialists, providing litigation capacity; (3) universal law school coverage with Article 14bis as mandatory curriculum in 100% of Argentine law faculties, providing reproduction capacity; (4) 185 bar associations with labor law specializations requiring 14bis mastery for certification, providing coordination capacity. II. THEORETICAL FOUNDATIONS: COGNITIVE ARCHITECTURE OF NORMATIVE PERCEPTION A. Hierarchical Predictive Coding and the Bayesian Brain The foundation for understanding institutional lock-in requires first establishing how individual brains generate normative perceptions. Dennett's synthesis of predictive coding neuroscience (2017) builds on seminal work by Friston (2010), Clark (2013, 2016), and Hohwy (2013) demonstrating that cortical processing operates through hierarchical prediction generation rather than through passive feature detection. The architecture inverts traditional models of perception: rather than sensory data flowing upward from receptors through increasingly abstract processing stages until reaching conscious awareness, the predictive coding brain continuously generates expectations that flow downward, using sensory input primarily to correct predictions when they fail. The mechanism operates through precision-weighted prediction errors. Each cortical level maintains probabilistic models of patterns at lower levels, generating predictions about expected activations. When lower-level activity deviates from predictions, error signals propagate upwardâ€”but their influence on belief revision depends critically on the precision (inverse variance) assigned to both the prediction and the incoming signal. High-precision priors resist updating even with substantial prediction errors because the system interprets errors as noise in the signal rather than as evidence against the prior. This creates the subjective experience Dennett terms "expecting ourselves to expect": when priors are strong, we perceive their confirmation as discovery of objective properties rather than as projection of internal models. Crucially, this architecture evolved not for epistemic accuracy but for pragmatic action-selection under resource constraints. As Dennett emphasizes, brains don't seek truthâ€”they seek survival-conducive action patterns that minimize long-term prediction error at acceptable metabolic cost. A prior that occasionally misrepresents reality but enables fast, low-cost responses will be maintained over a more accurate prior requiring expensive computation or revision. The beaver doesn't need accurate hydrological models to build

effective dams; it needs heuristics generating dam-building behavior reliably enough to outcompete alternative strategies in its ecological niche. Similarly, legal professionals don't need accurate institutional theories to defend constitutional provisions; they need priors generating defense behavior reliably enough to maintain professional legitimacy and institutional position. B. Friston's Free Energy Principle: Action and Perception as Dual Strategies Karl Friston's free energy framework (2010, 2013) formalizes the relationship between perception and action in Bayesian systems. The principle states that biological systems must minimize "free energy"â€”a quantity bounded above by "surprisal," the negative log-probability of sensory states given internal models. In practice, minimizing free energy means minimizing the difference between predicted and observed states. But critically, this minimization can occur through two mechanisms: Perceptual inference (belief updating): Revise internal models to better predict observations. If you expect coffee on the left but observe it on the right, update your belief about coffee's location. Active inference (world changing): Act to make observations match predictions. If you expect coffee on the left, move the cup leftward so future observations confirm the prediction. Individual organisms seamlessly integrate both strategies. When prediction errors arise, the brain computesâ€”implicitly, not consciouslyâ€”the expected free energy reduction from each strategy given current action costs and model revision costs. If perceptual inference is cheaper (weak prior, low revision cost), the system updates beliefs. If active inference is cheaper (strong prior, low action cost), the system changes the world. This creates the phenomenon of "motivated perception": strong priors resist disconfirmation not through motivated reasoning or cognitive bias but through Bayesian optimization selecting world-changing over belief-changing when energetically efficient. Friston's framework resolves the classical problem of action initiation in computational theories of mind. Purely inferential systemsâ€”those that only update beliefsâ€”face the problem of converting beliefs into actions. The free energy principle eliminates this dualism: action is prediction error minimization through environmental intervention, mechanically equivalent to perceptual inference but operating through different pathways. The beaver's dam-building is active inference minimizing prediction errors about water levels, offspring safety, and resource accessibility. The human's norm-enforcement is active inference

For institutional analysis, the critical insight is that action costs and revision costs differ dramatically between individual and collective systems. Individual brains update beliefs relatively cheaplyâ€”synaptic modification occurs continuously at minimal metabolic cost. But institutions update beliefs expensivelyâ€”coordinated revision across thousands of actors requires explicit communication, formal procedures, career disruption, and legitimacy management. This asymmetry creates a systematic bias toward institutional active inference when priors are strong, explaining the persistence of dysfunctional institutions that individual actors would quickly abandon. C. Bicchieri's Normative Expectations: The Social Dimension Cristina Bicchieri's work on normative expectations (2006, 2016) provides the social architecture bridging individual Bayesian cognition and collective institutional behavior. Bicchieri demonstrates that social norms persist not simply through behavioral observation but through layered expectation structures: Empirical expectations: beliefs about what others actually do (behavioral frequencies) Normative expectations: beliefs about what others think one ought to do (approval/disapproval patterns) Second-order normative expectations: beliefs about what others believe one ought to do (expected consensus) The power of norms derives not from the first layerâ€”actual behaviorâ€”but from the second and third layers, which concern reactions and meta-reactions. A norm prohibiting bribery persists not because bribery is rare (it may be common) but because individuals expect others to disapprove of bribery and expect that others share this expectation. These higher-order beliefs generate what Bicchieri terms "conditional preferences": agents prefer conformity when they believe others expect conformity and would sanction violations. This expectation architecture maps directly onto Heteronomous Bayesian Updating. When individuals observe reactions (normative expectations expressed through sanctions, disapproval, or praise), they perform Bayesian inference about the underlying norm's strength and scope. A single high-authority reaction (Supreme Court precedent, constitutional convention decision, bar association resolution) provides strong evidence about normative

Final Synthesis: Institutions as Bayesian Expectation Machines  The deepest insight from integrating Dennett's cognitive neuroscience with Dawkins' evolutionary biology and constitutional analysis is recognizing that legal institutions function as Bayesian expectation machines operating at civilizational scale. Just as individual brains minimize prediction error through hierarchical predictive coding, legal systems minimize institutional prediction error through distributed active inference. Just as brains project internal models onto the world and mistake projections for discoveries, legal communities project HBU-formed priors onto constitutional texts and mistake projections for foundational truths. Just as brains resist belief revision when action is cheaper, institutions resist reform when blocking costs less than coordinated updating. This reveals constitutional law as continuous with cognitive science and evolutionary biology rather than as separate magisterium governed by distinct principles. The mechanisms generating individual normative perception scale to institutional normative persistence through social transmission of reaction-based learning and extended phenotype construction of defense infrastructure.
