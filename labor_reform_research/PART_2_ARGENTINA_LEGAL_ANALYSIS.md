## I. THE BRAZILIAN PRECEDENT (2017)

### A. How Brazil Did It

**Pre-Reform Situation:**
- Brazil had ultraactivity doctrine similar to Argentina
- Article 7 of 1988 Constitution: Extensive list of worker rights (34 items)
- CLT (Consolida√ß√£o das Leis do Trabalho, 1943): Comprehensive labor code

**Reform Mechanism (Lei 13,467/2017):**

| Element | Brazilian Approach | Legal Basis |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Ultraactivity elimination** | Article 1 of Law 13,467 | STATUTORY change to CLT |
| **Constitutional challenge** | Filed at STF (Supreme Federal Court) | Multiple ADI (Direct Actions of Unconstitutionality) |
| **Outcome** | **UPHELD by STF** (2018-2024) | Majority ruled statute can modify procedural rules |

**Critical STF Distinction:**
- **Substantive rights** (minimum wage, maximum hours, severance) ‚Üí PROTECTED by Constitution
- **Procedural rules** (CBA duration, negotiation procedures) ‚Üí Subject to legislative modification

**Result:** Ultraactivity eliminated WITHOUT constitutional amendment.

### B. Constitutional Framing Strategy

**Brazilian Government's Argument (2017):**

> "Art. 7 CF/88 guarantees workers the right to collective bargaining, NOT the eternal validity of expired agreements. Ultraactivity is a judicial creation, not a constitutional mandate. Legislature can regulate negotiation procedures without violating substantive rights."

**STF Majority Opinion (Justice Fachin, 2019):**

> "The elimination of ultraactivity does not reduce workers' constitutional rights under Article 7. It merely changes the temporal validity of collective agreements. Workers retain the right to negotiate new agreements. This is a matter of labor procedure, not constitutional substance."

**Key elements of successful framing:**
1. ‚úÖ **Distinguished procedure from substance**
2. ‚úÖ **Preserved constitutional floor** (Article 7 minimums untouched)
3. ‚úÖ **Emphasized worker agency** ("choice to negotiate" vs. "imposed protection")
4. ‚úÖ **Temporary vs. permanent rights** (CBAs are contracts, not constitutional guarantees)
