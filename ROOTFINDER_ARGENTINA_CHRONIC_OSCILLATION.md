## II. ROOT 1 ‚Äî PROCEDURAL: JUDICIAL SUPREMACY WITHOUT ETERNITY CLAUSE

### A. Art. 116 and the Marbury Analog

Article 116 of the Argentine Constitution establishes the CSJN's constitutional supremacy:

> "Corresponde a la Corte Suprema y a los tribunales inferiores de la Naci√≥n, el conocimiento y decisi√≥n de todas las causas que versen sobre puntos regidos por la Constituci√≥n, y por las leyes de la Naci√≥n..."<sup>3</sup>

This provision, combined with Art. 31's supremacy clause ("Esta Constituci√≥n... son la ley suprema de la Naci√≥n"), creates an Argentine analog to *Marbury v. Madison* (1803). The CSJN possesses final interpretive authority over constitutional meaning, unreviewable by coordinate branches.<sup>4</sup> Unlike Germany's Federal Constitutional Court, whose jurisdiction is cabined by statute (Federal Constitutional Court Act of 1951), Argentina's CSJN exercises diffuse judicial review‚Äîany court may interpret the Constitution, with CSJN as hierarchical supreme authority.

**Doctrinal Flexibility**: The CSJN has repeatedly affirmed its power to reinterpret constitutional provisions without textual amendment, most explicitly in *Sim√≥n* (2005):

> "La Corte Suprema, en su car√°cter de √∫ltimo int√©rprete de la Constituci√≥n Nacional... puede apartarse de sus propios precedentes cuando existan fundamentos suficientes que justifiquen el cambio."<sup>5</sup>

Translation: "The Supreme Court, as final interpreter of the National Constitution... may depart from its own precedents when sufficient grounds justify the change."

This doctrinal posture mirrors U.S. *stare decisis* doctrine post-*Casey* (1992): precedents are entitled to respect but may be overruled when "grievously wrong" or "unworkable."<sup>6</sup> **Crucially, neither Argentina nor the United States possesses textual limits on constitutional reinterpretation**.

### B. Absence of Eternity Clause

Argentina's 1994 constitutional reform introduced Art. 30, governing amendment procedures, but **lacks an eternity clause** comparable to Germany's Basic Law Art. 79(3):

**Germany (Basic Law Art. 79-3)**: "Amendments to this Basic Law affecting the division of the Federation into L√§nder, their participation on principle in the legislative process, or the principles laid down in Articles 1 [human dignity] and 20 [democracy, rule of law, federal structure] shall be inadmissible."<sup>7</sup>

**Argentina (Art. 30 CN)**: "La Constituci√≥n puede reformarse en el todo o en cualquiera de sus partes. La necesidad de reforma debe ser declarada por el Congreso con el voto de dos terceras partes, al menos, de sus miembros; pero no se efectuar√° sino por una Convenci√≥n convocada al efecto."<sup>8</sup>

Translation: "The Constitution may be reformed in whole or in any of its parts." No substantive limitations exist beyond the procedural requirement of congressional supermajority (2/3) plus constituent convention.

**Comparative Implication**: Germany's Constitutional Court **cannot** interpret human dignity, democracy, or federalism out of existence‚Äîthese principles are immunized from judicial reinterpretation or formal amendment. Argentina's CSJN faces no analogous constraint. While certain scholars argue Art. 1 (republican form) and Art. 33 (unenumerated rights) function as implicit eternity clauses,<sup>9</sup> the Court has never enforced such limits. Indeed, the CSJN validated Menem's emergency decree system (1989-1999) despite Art. 76's express prohibition of legislative delegation in "penal, tax, electoral, or political party" matters‚Äîa constitutional provision effectively nullified by judicial interpretation.<sup>10</sup>

### C. Oscillation Cases: *Peralta* (1990) ‚Üí *Massa* (2006)

The CSJN's doctrinal oscillation is starkest in emergency powers jurisprudence:

**Era Menem**: ***Peralta v. Estado Nacional*** (1990)  
**Holding**: Presidential emergency decrees ("decretos de necesidad y urgencia") constitutionally valid under implied powers doctrine. President may unilaterally suspend contractual obligations (bank deposit confiscations/"Bonex Plan") during economic crisis.<sup>11</sup>

**Doctrine**: Extreme judicial deference. Court declared: "Cuando una situaci√≥n de necesidad p√∫blica exige medidas... se puede sin violar garant√≠as postergar obligaciones dentro de l√≠mites razonables."<sup>12</sup>

Translation: "When public necessity requires measures... [government] may postpone obligations without violating guarantees within reasonable limits."

**Era Kirchner**: ***Massa v. Poder Ejecutivo Nacional*** (2006)  
**Holding**: Presidential emergency decrees require **strict scrutiny**. Constitutional only if (1) extraordinary circumstances proven, (2) Congress cannot legislate in time, (3) decree subject to legislative ratification. Court invalidated decree adjusting public pensions, reversing *Peralta*'s permissive standard.<sup>13</sup>

**Doctrinal Shift**: From near-absolute deference (Menem era) to heightened review (Kirchner era) **without constitutional amendment**. Art. 99 inc. 3 (emergency decree provision) remained textually identical, yet interpretive stance reversed 180 degrees.

**Mechanism**: CSJN composition change: Menem Court (1990-2003) ‚Üí Kirchner Court (2004-2015). New justices (Zaffaroni, Lorenzetti, Highton de Nolasco, Argibay) explicitly rejected *Peralta* doctrine as inconsistent with post-1994 constitutional framework.

### D. Root 1 Conclusion: Procedural Vulnerability Present

**Argentina possesses Root 1 (Procedural)**: CSJN wields Marbury-style interpretive supremacy, unrestricted by eternity clauses or binding superprecedents. Constitutional meaning is judicially mutable. This is a **necessary** condition for change without amendment, but **insufficient** to explain oscillation rather than stable transformation.

## III. ROOT 2 ‚Äî ELECTORAL: APPOINTMENT CAPTURE VIA SUPERMAJORITY FAILURE

### A. Art. 99 inc. 4: The Two-Thirds Rule (And Its Failure)

Article 99 inc. 4 CN governs CSJN appointments:

> "Nombra los magistrados de la Corte Suprema con acuerdo del Senado por dos tercios de sus miembros presentes, en sesi√≥n p√∫blica, convocada al efecto."<sup>14</sup>

Translation: "President nominates CSJN justices with Senate agreement by two-thirds of members present, in public session convened for this purpose."

This **should** force bipartisan consensus, creating higher appointment friction than the U.S. system (simple majority post-2017 nuclear option).<sup>15</sup> Yet Argentina's experience demonstrates that **supermajority requirements function only when no single party controls ‚â•67% of the Senate**. When officialismo (governing party + allies) achieves two-thirds threshold, the constraint vanishes.

### B. Era Menem (1990-1999): Court-Packing via Supermajority Control

**Context**: President Carlos Menem (Partido Justicialista/PJ) inherited a five-member CSJN in 1989, perceived as aligned with outgoing Radical government.<sup>16</sup> Menem's strategy: expand Court to nine members, fill with loyalists.

**Legislative Capture**:
- **April 16, 1990**: Congress passes Law 23.774, expanding CSJN from 5 to 9 justices.<sup>17</sup>
- **Senate Composition 1990**: PJ controlled 41 of 72 seats (56.9%). Not enough for 2/3 alone (48 votes required).
- **Coalitional Majority**: PJ + provincial parties (UCeDe, Partido Autonomista, defectors) ‚Üí achieved 2/3 threshold.

**Appointments 1990-1994**:
- **April 18, 1990**: Menem nominates four justices (Nazareno, Molin√© O'Connor, Belluscio, Barra) ‚Üí Senate confirms 54-14 (79.4% approval).<sup>18</sup>
- **1993**: Additional appointments (L√≥pez, V√°zquez, Boggiano) ‚Üí Senate confirms with similar supermajority.
- **Result**: By 1994, Menem appointed **6 of 9 justices** (66.7% of Court).

**"Mayor√≠a Autom√°tica"**: The five justices (Nazareno, Molin√© O'Connor, L√≥pez, V√°zquez, Boggiano) formed automatic majority voting with government 93% of the time in high-stakes economic cases.<sup>19</sup> Dissenting justices (Petracchi, Fayt, Belluscio occasional) could not prevent doctrinal consolidation.

### C. Era Kirchner (2003-2015): Court Purge via Juicio Pol√≠tico

**Context**: President N√©stor Kirchner (PJ) faced Menem's CSJN majority. Strategy: forced resignations + juicio pol√≠tico (impeachment).

**Political Pressure Campaign**:

- **October 22, 2003**: Justice L√≥pez resigns under similar pressure.

**Result**: Four of five "mayor√≠a autom√°tica" justices removed/resigned by 2005.

**New Appointments (2003-2009)**:
Kirchner nominated four justices (Zaffaroni, Lorenzetti, Highton de Nolasco, Argibay) with **apparent Senate supermajority** (PJ + allies controlled ~50 of 72 seats, needed 48 for 2/3). However, **effective control via Peronist discipline** ensured confirmations:<sup>22</sup>

### D. Era Macri (2015-2019): Divided Government, Limited Appointments

**Context**: President Mauricio Macri (Cambiemos/PRO coalition) faced divided Senate‚Äîno party held >40% of seats.

**Senate Composition 2016-2019**:
- PRO + allies: ~34 seats (47%)
- Peronist factions: ~33 seats (46%)

**Result**: Macri nominated only **2 justices** in 4 years (Rosatti 2016, Rosenkrantz 2017), both requiring negotiated bipartisan support:

**Capture Blocked**: Without Senate control, Macri could not reshape Court majority. Kirchnerist justices (Lorenzetti, Zaffaroni [retired 2014], Highton) retained influence.

### E. Root 2 Conclusion: Electoral Vulnerability Present (But Intermittent)

**Argentina possesses Root 2 (Electoral)**: Presidential appointment power + court tenure (vitalicio until age 75) + narrow confirmation process. **But** supermajority requirement (2/3 Senate) creates **higher friction than U.S. system**:

## IV. ROOT 3 ‚Äî MEMETIC: ABSENCE OF STABLE DOCTRINAL PIPELINE

### A. The Missing Federalist Society: Organizational Void

The United States' constitutional stability (despite electoral capture) derives from **memetic infrastructure**: Federalist Society provides generational continuity of originalist theory, recruitment pipeline (law school ‚Üí clerkships ‚Üí federal bench), and ideological coherence across Republican administrations.<sup>24</sup> Argentina **lacks** any comparable institution.

**Organizational Landscape Argentina**:

**Progressive/Human Rights**:
- **CELS** (Centro de Estudios Legales y Sociales): Founded 1979, human rights litigation, no judicial recruitment function.<sup>25</sup>
- **ADC** (Asociaci√≥n por los Derechos Civiles): Founded 1995, civil rights advocacy, academic but not pipeline-building.

**Conservative/Economic Liberalism**:
- **Fundaci√≥n Libertad** (Rosario): Think-tank promoting market economy, loose affiliation with judges, no systematic pipeline.

**Professional Associations**:
- **Colegio de Abogados de la Ciudad de Buenos Aires** (CAL): Professional bar, no doctrinal alignment.

**Critical Absence**: None of these organizations perform Federalist Society's function‚Äî**ideological vetting + judicial recruitment + doctrinal socialization**. Argentine judges' constitutional philosophies reflect personal academic training, not organizational indoctrination.

### B. Doctrinal Discontinuity by Era: Four Constitutional Philosophies in 35 Years

Argentina's constitutional discourse exhibits radical discontinuity across presidential administrations:

#### Era Menem (1990-1999): Positivist Economic Liberalism

**Theoretical Foundation**: Kelsenian positivism + Chicago School law & economics.  
**Key Justices**:
- **Belluscio**: Legal positivist, deference to executive economic policy.
- **Nazareno**: Business-friendly, validated privatizations and emergency decrees.

**Representative Doctrine**: *Peralta* (1990) emergency powers, *Aerol√≠neas Argentinas* (1992) privatization validation, *YPF* (1993) state enterprise dissolution.

**Academic Alignment**: Minimal. Few law professors supported *Peralta* doctrine; Court operated counter to academic consensus.

#### Era Kirchner (2003-2015): Guarantismo + International Human Rights Law

**Theoretical Foundation**: Luigi Ferrajoli's guarantismo, Inter-American Human Rights Court supremacy.  
**Key Justices**:
- **Zaffaroni**: Criminal law scholar, guarantist philosophy, rejected Menem's positivism.
- **Lorenzetti**: "Social constitutionalism," rights maximization over economic efficiency.

**Doctrinal Reversal**: *Sim√≥n* (2005) declared unconstitutional amnesty laws (Full Stop/Due Obedience), reversing prior precedent. Court held international human rights law (jus cogens) supersedes domestic amnesty legislation.<sup>26</sup>

**Citation Pattern**: *Sim√≥n* cited **zero** Menem-era precedents as authority. Instead cited European Court of Human Rights, Inter-American Court, UN Human Rights Committee. Doctrinal genealogy **completely severed** from prior era.

#### Era Macri (2015-2019): Institutionalist Rule of Law

**Theoretical Foundation**: Liberal institutionalism, federalism revival, separation of powers.  
**Key Justices**:
- **Rosatti**: Federalist scholar, emphasized provincial autonomy.
- **Rosenkrantz**: Textualist leanings (partial originalism), institutional competence focus.

**Doctrinal Shift**: Reduced deference to international human rights courts (partial rollback of *Sim√≥n* logic), strengthened federalism in tax disputes, reasserted judicial review limits.

**Citation Pattern**: Rosatti/Rosenkrantz opinions cited pre-Kirchner precedents (1960s-1980s) **skipping over Kirchner era entirely**.

#### Era Milei (2023-present): Emergent Libertarian Constitutionalism

**Theoretical Foundation**: Rothbard/Hayek libertarianism, "anarcho-capitalist" constitutionalism (hypothesized).  
**Current Court**: Holdovers from Kirchner (Lorenzetti, Rosatti, Rosenkrantz). Milei has not yet made appointments (CSJN currently 3 justices, 2 vacancies).

**Expected Doctrine** (if Milei fills vacancies): Minimal state doctrine, property rights absolutism, possible revival of *Lochner*-era economic liberty. Would **reject both** Kirchner guarantismo and Macri institutionalism.

### D. Root 3 Conclusion: Memetic Vulnerability Absent

**Argentina lacks Root 3 (Memetic)**: No organizational infrastructure reinforces doctrinal continuity. ## VII. POLICY IMPLICATIONS: IMMUN

IZING AGAINST OSCILLATION

### A. Why Current Design Fails

**Root 1 (Procedural) + Root 2 (Electoral) + No Root 3 (Memetic) = Oscillation Trap**

Argentina's constitutional architecture permits radical change without amendment (good for adaptability, bad for stability) but lacks mechanisms to consolidate change into durable equilibrium. Each capture event resets doctrinal baseline.

### B. Three Reform Paths (Ordered by Feasibility)

#### **Option 1: Build Memetic Infrastructure** (Medium-Term, Low Feasibility)

**Mechanism**: Create Argentine equivalent of Federalist Society‚Äîideologically neutral but doctrine-specific legal networks.

**Example**: "Red Argentina de Constitucionalismo Federal" (center-right) + "Red Argentina de Constitucionalismo Social" (center-left). Both provide:
- Law school chapters teaching coherent constitutional theory
- Clerkship pipelines reinforcing doctrinal training
- Post-appointment seminars maintaining ideological commitment

**Challenge**: Requires 15-20 years to bear fruit (Federalist Society founded 1982, achieved SCOTUS capture 2016-2020). Argentina's political culture (personalist, not institutional) resists long-term investment in ideological infrastructure.

#### **Option 2: Electoral Safeguards** (Short-Term, High Feasibility)

**Mechanism 1: Staggered 18-Year Terms** (constitutional amendment Art. 99)  
- Each justice serves single 18-year term, non-renewable.
- Staggered appointments: 1 vacancy every 2 years ‚Üí president needs 8+ years to flip Court majority.
- **Precedent**: Mexico 1994 reform (15-year terms) stabilized SCJN for 30 years.<sup>30</sup>

**Mechanism 2: Expand Court to 15 Members**  
- Dilutes individual appointment impact (1/15 < 1/5).
- Requires sustained Senate supermajority across 4-6 appointments to capture.
- **Downside**: May increase deliberation costs, fragment jurisprudence further.

**Mechanism 3: Raise Senate Threshold to 75% (54/72 votes)**  
- Forces broader coalitional consensus than current 2/3 (48/72).
- **Precedent**: None in presidential systems, but parliamentary democracies use qualified majorities for constitutional courts (e.g., Spain requires 3/5 Congress + 3/5 Senate for Constitutional Court appointments).

#### **Option 3: Partial Eternity Clause** (Long-Term, Medium Feasibility)

**Mechanism**: Constitutional amendment adding Art. 30bis:

> "The following principles are irreformable and judicially unalterable: (1) republican form of government (Art. 1), (2) federal structure (Art. 5-128), (3) separation of powers (Art. 1), (4) human dignity and fundamental rights (Art. 14-43). No constitutional interpretation by the CSJN shall vitiate these principles."

**Effect**: Limits oscillation to **non-core** doctrines. Emergency powers (*Peralta*/*Massa*) could still oscillate, but e.g., CSJN could not interpret away federalism or habeas corpus.

**Challenge**: Requires constitutional convention (Art. 30: 2/3 Congress + convention). Argentina's last convention (1994) took 5 years, was highly contentious.

## VIII. CONCLUSION: OSCILLATION AS DESIGN FAILURE, NOT INEVITABILITY

Argentina's chronic constitutional oscillation is **neither inevitable nor mysterious**. RootFinder analysis reveals precise causal mechanism:

**Root 1 (Procedural) present** ‚Üí Change without amendment possible  
**Root 2 (Electoral) present but intermittent** ‚Üí Capture requires supermajority coalitions (8-12 year cycles)  
**Root 3 (Memetic) absent** ‚Üí Captured justices lack doctrinal continuity with predecessors  
**Result** ‚Üí Oscillation, not transformation

This contrasts with:
- **United States**: All 3 roots present ‚Üí stable transformation (originalist hegemony consolidated despite legitimation crisis)
- **Chile**: Roots 1-3 contested ‚Üí change impossible (double constitutional rejection)
- **Germany**: Root 1 absent (eternity clause) + Root 2 constrained (staggered terms) ‚Üí stability via immunization

**Theoretical Contribution**: Elite Cohesion Index (ECI) is **necessary but insufficient** for constitutional stability. High ECI without memetic infrastructure produces **tactical coherence within eras, strategic fragmentation across eras**. Argentina's ECI 0.72 measures coordination capacity, not stability.
