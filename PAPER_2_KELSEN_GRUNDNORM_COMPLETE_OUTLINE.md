# Paper 2: Resolving Kelsen's Paradox Through Evolutionary Biology

## Extended Abstract (500 words)

For nearly a century, Hans Kelsen's *Pure Theory of Law* has dominated legal philosophy through its rigorous separation of law from morality, politics, and sociology. Yet Kelsen himself acknowledged a fundamental paradox at the heart of his system: the problem of the *Grundnorm* (basic norm). 

Every legal norm derives its validity from a higher norm in Kelsen's hierarchical structure. Constitutional provisions validate statutes, statutes validate regulations, and so forth. But this regress must terminate somewhere. Kelsen posited the Grundnorm—a presupposed, non-positive norm that commands obedience to the constitution—as the ultimate foundation of legal validity. Crucially, Kelsen admitted this norm is neither enacted nor justified; it is simply **presupposed as a transcendental condition** for legal thought.

This "solution" has generated 80 years of philosophical debate without resolution. Hart criticized it as a "verbal maneuver," Raz questioned its necessity, and Alexy attempted procedural alternatives. All failed to resolve the core problem: **Where does the normative force of the Grundnorm itself come from?**

This paper presents an empirical resolution to Kelsen's paradox using Extended Phenotype Theory (EPT) from evolutionary biology. Our central thesis: **The Grundnorm is not presupposed—it is evolutionarily inherited.**

Drawing on converging evidence from four independent disciplines—primatology, archaeology, developmental psychology, and neuroscience—we demonstrate that normative cognition predates linguistic articulation by millions of years. Chimpanzees enforce proto-norms without language (Riedl et al. 2012), Paleolithic humans practiced normative exclusion 25,000 years before agriculture (Pettitt 2013), human infants display fairness intuitions before acquiring deontic vocabulary (Hamann et al. 2011), and neural circuits for norm processing evolved before language-specialized cortical regions (Moll et al. 2008).

These findings refute Kelsen's implicit assumption that normativity requires linguistic articulation. Instead, we propose that the "presupposed" Grundnorm reflects genetically and culturally inherited predispositions for norm-following that evolved through multilevel selection. Groups with proto-normative systems—mechanisms for detecting violations, punishing defectors, and rewarding cooperators—systematically outcompeted groups without such systems across the Pleistocene.

The normative "ought-feeling" that Kelsen described as presupposed is actually an **adaptive inheritance**, analogous to Chomsky's Universal Grammar. We do not "agree" to follow grammatical rules any more than we "agree" to follow the Grundnorm—both are inherited cognitive structures that enable coordination at scale.

This evolutionary resolution has profound implications for legal theory:

1. **Validity redefined**: Legal validity depends not on hierarchical derivation from a presupposed norm, but on memetic alignment with inherited proto-norms. This explains why some formally valid laws generate widespread "moral resistance" (they conflict with proto-norms like fairness or reciprocity).

2. **International law explained**: Treaties lacking enforcement mechanisms can nonetheless "feel binding" because they activate inherited norm-following predispositions, independent of sanction.

3. **Constitutional interpretation**: Constitutions that violate proto-normative principles (e.g., denial of reciprocity, extreme inequality) generate predictable patterns of non-compliance, regardless of formal validity.

4. **Legal transplants**: Why certain laws "fail to take root" in different cultures becomes explicable through gene-culture coevolution theory.

This paper does not commit the naturalistic fallacy. We are not deriving *ought* from *is*, but rather explaining the psychological origin of the *ought-feeling* that makes legal systems possible. The Grundnorm is real—but it is not presupposed. It is inherited.

## Key Definitions (To Be Established in Section 1)

### Grundnorm (Kelsen's Definition)
> "The basic norm is not created in a legal procedure by a law-creating organ. It is not—as a positive legal norm is—valid because it is created in a certain way by a legal act, but it is valid because it is presupposed to be valid" (Kelsen, *Pure Theory of Law*, 1967, p. 201).

### Extended Phenotype (Dawkins, 1982)
> "An animal's behavior tends to maximize the survival of the genes 'for' that behavior, whether or not those genes happen to be in the body of the particular animal performing it" (Dawkins, *The Extended Phenotype*, p. 233).

### Proto-Norm (Our Definition)
> A behavioral regularity enforced through partner choice, coalition formation, or punishment that stabilizes cooperation in social groups, operating without linguistic articulation of deontic rules.

### Normative Inheritance (Our Definition)
> The transmission of predispositions for norm-detection, norm-following, and norm-enforcement through both genetic and cultural channels, shaped by multilevel selection across evolutionary time.

### Memetic Alignment (Our Definition)
> The degree to which a formally articulated legal norm (meme) corresponds to evolutionarily inherited proto-normative predispositions (genes + cultural transmission). High alignment → spontaneous compliance; Low alignment → resistance/enforcement problems.

## SECTION 2: The Pure Theory and Its Discontents (10 pages)

### 2.1 Kelsen's Pure Theory: Core Commitments
**Content**:
- **Purity**: Separation of law from morality, politics, sociology, psychology
- **Normativity**: Law consists of *ought* statements, not *is* statements
- **Hierarchy**: Legal norms form pyramidal structure (Stufenbau)
- **Validity**: Each norm derives validity from higher norm
- **Basic Norm**: Ultimate foundation as presupposition

**Key texts**:
- *Pure Theory of Law* (1934, 1967 editions)
- *General Theory of Law and State* (1945)
- Compare evolution of Kelsen's position across editions

### 2.2 The Grundnorm in Detail
**Content**:
- **Formulation**: "One ought to obey the commands of the constitution-makers"
- **Characteristics**:
  - Not positive law (not enacted)
  - Not derived from higher norm (would restart regress)
  - Not moral principle (would violate purity)
  - Not factual statement (would violate is/ought distinction)
- **Status**: "Transcendental-logical presupposition" 
- **Function**: Makes legal cognition possible (analogous to Kant's categories)

**Critical passage** (Pure Theory, 1967, p. 204):
> "The basic norm is not valid because it has been created in a certain way, but its validity is presupposed, and it is presupposed because without this presupposition no human act could be interpreted as a legal, especially as a norm-creating, act."

### 2.3 Internal Tensions in Kelsen's Account
**Content**:

#### Tension 1: Is Grundnorm a Norm?
- If YES: What makes it normative (source of its validity)?
- If NO: How can it ground normativity of other norms?
- **Kelsen's response**: It's a "presupposition," not a norm (but then how does it create normativity?)

#### Tension 2: Epistemological vs. Ontological Status
- Sometimes Kelsen treats Grundnorm as **epistemological**: Condition for legal knowledge
- Other times as **ontological**: Foundation of legal validity
- These are not the same thing
- **Problem**: Which is it?

#### Tension 3: Necessity vs. Contingency
- Kelsen says Grundnorm is "presupposed" (suggests necessity)
- But specific content varies by legal system (suggests contingency)
- **Question**: What is necessary—the act of presupposing, or the specific norm presupposed?

### 2.4 Kelsen's Awareness of the Problem
**Content**:
- Quote Kelsen's admission of difficulty (*Allgemeine Theorie*, 1925)
- His 1964 essay "On the Concept of Norm" shows continued struggle
- Letter to Alf Ross (1960s) acknowledging "mystery" of Grundnorm
- **Key point**: Kelsen knew this was problematic but saw no alternative

### 2.5 Why Philosophical Repair Attempts Failed
**Preview**:
- Hart's "social rule of recognition" (just moves problem one step)
- Raz's "sources thesis" (assumes what it needs to prove)
- Alexy's "discourse theory" (requires ideal speech conditions)
- Dworkin's "constructive interpretation" (smuggles normativity through "best light")

**Transition**: "All these solutions presuppose normative commitment. None explain where normative commitment comes from."

## SECTION 3: Failed Philosophical Resolutions (8 pages)

### 3.1 Hart's Social Rule of Recognition
**Content**:
- Hart's critique: Grundnorm is "needless shuffle" (*Concept of Law*, 1961, p. 292)
- Hart's alternative: Rule of recognition as social fact
- Officials accept rule from "internal point of view"

**Problem**:
- What grounds the normative force of "internal point of view"?
- Hart says it's just a fact that officials accept it
- But this collapses *ought* into *is*
- Hart hasn't solved Kelsen's problem—he's denied there's a problem

**Quote Hart** (Concept of Law, p. 110):
> "The rule of recognition exists only as a complex, but normally concordant, practice"

**Our response**: But why should we follow this practice? Regress returns.

### 3.2 Raz's Exclusionary Reasons
**Content**:
- Raz: Law claims legitimate authority
- Authority provides "exclusionary reasons" (reasons not to act on other reasons)
- Legal validity grounded in rational agency

**Problem**:
- Why should we accept exclusionary reasons as binding?
- Raz: Because authority helps us comply with reasons we already have
- **But**: This presumes we have antecedent normative commitments
- Hasn't explained where those commitments originate

**Quote Raz** (*Authority of Law*, 1979, p. 28):
> "The exercise of authority is justified if it helps the subjects better comply with reasons which apply to them"

**Our response**: Granted—but what makes those reasons apply? Regress continues.

### 3.3 Alexy's Discourse Theory
**Content**:
- Alexy: Legal validity requires "correctness claim"
- Correctness justified through rational discourse under ideal conditions
- Habermasian proceduralism applied to law

**Problem**:
- Ideal speech conditions never obtain in reality
- Even if they did, why are procedures normatively binding?
- **Circular**: Discourse participants must already accept norms of discourse
- These norms themselves require justification

**Quote Alexy** (*Concept and Validity of Law*, 1992, p. 212):
> "A norm is correct if and only if it can be the result of a rational discourse"

**Our response**: What makes discourse rational? More norms. Regress.

### 3.4 Dworkin's Constructive Interpretation
**Content**:
- Dworkin: Law is "interpretive concept"
- Interpretation aims to show legal practice in "best light"
- Integrity as foundational value

**Problem**:
- What counts as "best light"? Requires antecedent normative commitments
- Why is integrity a value? Dworkin simply asserts it
- **Dworkin's advantage**: Openly rejects fact/value distinction
- **Dworkin's problem**: Doesn't explain where values come from

**Quote Dworkin** (*Law's Empire*, 1986, p. 52):
> "Constructive interpretation is a matter of imposing purpose on an object or practice"

**Our response**: Whose purpose? On what authority? Grounds missing.

### 3.5 Natural Law Responses
**Content**:
- Finnis: Basic goods known through practical reason
- Fuller: Internal morality of law (procedural natural law)
- Aquinas-inspired theories: Eternal law → natural law → human law

**Problem with Finnis**:
- Basic goods are just asserted, not derived
- Appeals to "self-evident" practical reason
- But what makes something self-evident? Subjective intuition?

**Problem with Fuller**:
- Procedural constraints (generality, clarity, etc.) assume antecedent commitment to rule of law
- Why value rule of law? Fuller can't say without circularity

**Problem with Theistic Natural Law**:
- Requires theology (Divine Will as ultimate ground)
- Not available in secular legal systems
- Even if God exists, Euthyphro problem: Is it good because God wills it, or does God will it because it's good?

### 3.6 Why All Solutions Fail: The Common Pattern
**Content**:
- **Diagnosis**: All theories presuppose normative commitment somewhere
- Hart: "Internal point of view"
- Raz: Accepting exclusionary reasons
- Alexy: Accepting discourse norms
- Dworkin: Valuing integrity
- Finnis: Recognizing basic goods
- **Pattern**: Normative foundation smuggled in, not explained

**Key insight**: Philosophy alone cannot resolve this. Need empirical account of where normative psychology originates.

**Transition**: "What if the Grundnorm is not a presupposition but a biological inheritance?"

## SECTION 4: Extended Phenotype Theory Applied to Norms (8 pages)

### 4.1 Dawkins' Extended Phenotype Concept
**Content**:
- Standard phenotype: Traits expressed in organism's body (eye color, height)
- **Extended phenotype**: Traits expressed outside organism's body
- Classic examples:
  - Beaver dam (gene expression in environment)
  - Spider web (extended structure built by genes)
  - Cuckoo manipulation of host behavior
  
**Key principle** (Dawkins, 1982, p. 233):
> "An animal's behavior tends to maximize the survival of the genes 'for' that behavior, whether or not those genes happen to be in the body of the particular animal performing it"

### 4.2 From Physical Structures to Social Structures
**Content**:
- Extended phenotypes need not be physical
- **Social structures** can be extended phenotypes:
  - Dominance hierarchies
  - Cooperative alliances
  - Territorial boundaries
  
**Criterion**: Structure reliably increases fitness of genes that code for structure-building behavior

**Application to law**: Legal institutions as extended phenotypes of norm-building genes

### 4.3 Proto-Norms as Precursors to Legal Norms
**Content**:

**Definition of proto-norm**:
- Behavioral regularity enforced through social mechanisms
- No linguistic articulation required
- Functions to stabilize cooperation

**Mechanisms of proto-norm enforcement**:
1. **Partner choice** (Melis & Tomasello, 2013): Avoid defectors in future interactions
2. **Coalition formation** (Boehm, 1999): Subordinates collectively suppress dominance
3. **Reactive punishment** (Jensen, 2010): Second-party retaliation against cheaters
4. **Third-party punishment** (rare in non-humans, but see Leimgruber et al. 2016)

**Key point**: These mechanisms require no deontic language ("You must share"), only behavioral patterns shaped by selection

### 4.4 Gene-Culture Coevolution of Normative Capacity
**Content**:
- Genetic component: 
  - Predispositions for fairness detection (inequity aversion)
  - Punishment propensity (altruistic punishment)
  - Norm-following tendencies (conformist bias)
  
- Cultural component:
  - Specific norm content varies by group
  - Transmitted through social learning
  - Rapid evolution (generations, not millennia)
  
- **Interaction**: Genes create capacity for norm-learning; culture provides specific norms; successful norms feed back to genetic selection

**Model**: Dual inheritance theory (Boyd & Richerson, 1985)

### 4.5 Multilevel Selection and Norm Evolution
**Content**:
- **Individual level**: Free-riders have advantage within group
- **Group level**: Groups with norm-enforcers outcompete groups without
- **Result**: Norm-enforcing traits maintained despite individual cost

**Formal condition** (Bowles & Gintis, 2011):
- Between-group variance in cooperative norms > Within-group disadvantage of norm-followers
- This condition met during Pleistocene (small groups, frequent intergroup competition)

**Archaeological evidence**:
- Differential group survival (Shennan, 2011)
- Demographic bottlenecks favor cooperation (Henrich, 2004)

### 4.6 From Proto-Norms to Articulated Law: The Role of Language
**Content**:
- **Proto-norm stage** (6 Ma - 200 Ka): Enforcement through partner choice, coalitions
- **Deontic language stage** (200 Ka - 10 Ka): Linguistic articulation enables:
  - Explicit formulation of norms
  - Transmission to strangers
  - Scaling beyond Dunbar's number (~150)
  - Third-party enforcement
  
- **Written law stage** (5 Ka - present): Further scaling, institutional enforcement

**Critical point**: Language didn't create normativity—it scaffolded pre-existing normative psychology

### 4.7 The Grundnorm as Inherited Predisposition
**Content**:
- Kelsen's Grundnorm = Linguistic articulation of inherited norm-following predisposition
- It "feels" presupposed because we're born with it (like grammar)
- It's not arbitrary because it reflects millions of years of selection
- It's not justified because adaptations don't require justification

**Analogy to Universal Grammar** (Chomsky, 1965):
- Children don't "choose" to follow grammatical rules
- They inherit capacity to acquire grammar
- Similarly: Adults don't "choose" to accept Grundnorm
- They inherit predisposition to accept norm-structures

**Key difference from Kelsen**: 
- Kelsen: Grundnorm is transcendental presupposition
- EPT: Grundnorm is evolutionary inheritance

## SECTION 5: Empirical Evidence Against Presupposition (15 pages)

### 5.1 Overview: Four Independent Evidence Streams
**Content**:
- **Convergent validation**: Four disciplines independently show normativity predates language
- **Phylogenetic evidence**: Primatology (proto-norms in apes)
- **Archaeological evidence**: Pre-linguistic normative exclusion
- **Ontogenetic evidence**: Developmental psychology (fairness before deontic language)
- **Neurobiological evidence**: Brain circuits for norms predate language circuits

**Methodological note**: Each evidence stream independently refutes "presupposition" account

### 5.2 Primatological Evidence: Proto-Norms Without Language

#### 5.2.1 Chimpanzee Partner Choice (Melis & Tomasello, 2013)
**Content**:
- **N=12 chimpanzees**, dyadic cooperation task
- Chimpanzees preferentially chose partners who shared food equitably in previous interactions
- **Mechanism**: Social selection, not punishment
- **Interpretation**: Proto-norm of reciprocity enforced through partner choice
- **Implication**: Normative enforcement doesn't require language or third-party punishment

**Key quote** (Melis & Tomasello, 2013, p. 2137):
> "Chimpanzees' cooperation is regulated by a form of partner choice based on previous interactions"

#### 5.2.2 Absence of Third-Party Punishment (Riedl et al., 2012)
**Content**:
- **N=13 chimpanzees**, theft scenario with third-party observer
- **Result**: 0% third-party punishment (TPP) across 60 trials
- **Contrast**: Human children show ~60-80% TPP by age 8
- **Interpretation**: TPP requires linguistic articulation of norms (deontic reasoning)
- **Implication**: Partner choice is phylogenetically older enforcement mechanism

**Critical point**: Proto-norms exist WITHOUT TPP (most sophisticated form of enforcement)

#### 5.2.3 Capuchin Inequity Punishment (Leimgruber et al., 2016)  
**Content**:
- **N=6 brown capuchin monkeys**, inequity aversion test
- **Result**: 25.7% punishment of inequitable distributors
- **Critical detail**: Punishment is **intention-blind** (capuchins punish even accidental inequity)
- **Interpretation**: Spiteful reaction to unfairness, not normative judgment
- **Implication**: Intention-sensitivity requires deontic language

**Developmental prediction**: Human children should show capuchin-like intention-blind punishment before age 3, transitioning to intention-sensitive punishment after deontic language acquisition (age 5-7)

#### 5.2.4 Bonobo Coalition Suppression (Surbeck et al., 2025)
**Content**:
- **N=6 bonobo communities**, long-term observation
- **Result**: Female coalitions suppress male dominance (β=0.762, p<0.001)
- **Mechanism**: Coordinated aggression toward would-be alpha males
- **Interpretation**: Proto-normative enforcement of egalitarianism
- **Implication**: Boehm's Reverse Dominance Hierarchy operates without language

**Connection to Grundnorm**: The "norm of egalitarianism" enforced by bonobos is not articulated—it's a stable behavioral equilibrium shaped by selection

### 5.3 Archaeological Evidence: Pre-Linguistic Normative Exclusion

#### 5.3.1 Temporal Increase in Social Exclusion (Pettitt, 2013)
**Content**:
- **Dataset**: 3,902 Paleolithic-to-Chalcolithic burials (40,000 BP - 3,000 BP)
- **Measure**: Frequency of burials excluded from main cemetery
- **Results**:
  - Paleolithic (40,000-10,000 BP): 0.67% exclusion
  - Mesolithic: 2.5% exclusion
  - Neolithic: 9.0% exclusion  
  - Chalcolithic: 27.0% exclusion
- **Statistical significance**: χ²=11.89, p<0.001
- **Timeline**: Exclusion increases 40-fold over 37,000 years

**Key interpretation**: 
- 0.67% Paleolithic exclusion suggests proto-normative exclusion before complex language
- Increase correlates with agricultural transition (10,000 BP), population density, resource defensibility
- **NOT** with language emergence (which stabilized ~200,000 BP)

#### 5.3.2 Sunghir Children: Anti-Exclusion Proto-Norm (Trinkaus & Buzhilova, 2012)
**Content**:
- **Site**: Sunghir, Russia (~34,000 BP, Upper Paleolithic)
- **Burial**: Two disabled children (Sunghir 2: ankylosing spondylitis; Sunghir 3: femoral curvature)
- **Grave goods**: >10,000 mammoth ivory beads, Arctic fox teeth, ivory spears
- **Labor investment**: ~7,500 hours for bead production alone
- **Interpretation**: Community invested extraordinary resources in non-productive members

**Implications**:
1. Proto-norm AGAINST exclusion of disabled
2. Operated 34,000 years ago (before agriculture, before cities, before writing)
3. Refutes notion that "normative inclusion" requires articulated moral philosophy

**Connection to Grundnorm**: The "ought" to care for disabled group members existed without linguistic articulation

### 5.4 Developmental Psychology: Ontogenetic Emergence of Normativity

#### 5.4.1 Pre-Deontic Fairness (Hamann et al., 2011)
**Content**:
- **N=48 human children**, age 3 years
- **Task**: Collaborative resource acquisition, then division
- **Result**: Children share equitably with collaborative partners (not with strangers)
- **Critical timing**: Age 3 = BEFORE full deontic language competence (which emerges age 5-7)
- **Interpretation**: Fairness intuitions predate linguistic articulation of "ought"

**Quote** (Hamann et al., 2011, p. 328):
> "Even 3-year-old children already take into account their partner's contribution when dividing spoils"

**Implication**: Norm-following doesn't require ability to articulate norms

#### 5.4.2 Deontic Language Acquisition Timeline (Choi, 2006)
**Content**:
- **Study**: Longitudinal acquisition of modal verbs in English and Korean
- **Finding**: Deontic modals ("must," "should," "may") emerge age 5-7
- **Earlier**: Children use non-deontic language for same functions (imperatives, desires)
- **Gap**: 3-5 years where fairness behavior exists WITHOUT deontic language

**Key point**: The developmental gap proves normative psychology ≠ deontic language

#### 5.4.3 Developmental Prediction (Testable)
**Content**:
- **Hypothesis**: Children should show intention-blind punishment (capuchin-like) age 2-3
- **Transition**: Intention-sensitive punishment should emerge age 5-7 (with deontic language)
- **Mechanism**: Deontic language enables mental state attribution for normative judgment

**Falsification criterion**: If intention-sensitivity appears BEFORE deontic language mastery, this would challenge EPT claim that language scaffolds sophisticated normative cognition

### 5.5 Neuroscientific Evidence: Phylogenetic Priority of Norm-Processing Circuits

### Objection Category 1: Methodological

| Objection | Source | Our Response | Evidence to Deploy |
|-----------|--------|--------------|-------------------|
| **"Naturalistic fallacy"** | Hume, Moore | Explaining ≠ justifying; we explain ought-feeling, not derive ought from is | Analogy to pareidolia (explaining face-detection ≠ justifying belief in cloud-faces) |

| Objection | Source | Our Response | Evidence to Deploy |
|-----------|--------|--------------|-------------------|

### Opening Strategy: Establish Problem as Urgent
**Technique**: Start with concrete legal example (Supreme Court authority regress)
**Goal**: Show this isn't abstract philosophy—it's about legitimacy of law itself
**Avoid**: Dense philosophical jargon in first 2 pages

**Technique**: Quote Kelsen extensively, show evolution of his thinking across 40 years
**Goal**: Demonstrate we understand Kelsen better than critics who dismiss him
**Avoid**: Straw-manning Kelsen (he's actually quite sophisticated)

### Section 3 Strategy: Generous to Alternatives
**Technique**: Cite each alternative theory's strongest formulation, acknowledge insights
**Goal**: Show we've engaged alternatives fairly before rejecting them
**Avoid**: Dismissive tone (these are serious theories by brilliant philosophers)

### Section 4 Strategy: Make EPT Accessible
**Technique**: Use concrete examples (beaver dam, spider web) before abstract application
**Goal**: Legal philosophers may not know evolutionary biology—make it intuitive
**Avoid**: Assuming familiarity with extended phenotype concept

### Section 5 Strategy: Overwhelm with Convergent Evidence
**Technique**: Four independent disciplines, each sufficient alone, devastating together
**Goal**: Make empirical case undeniable (even if theoretical interpretation contested)
**Avoid**: Cherry-picking studies—acknowledge limitations honestly

### Section 6 Strategy: Show This Solves Kelsen's Exact Problem
**Technique**: Return to Kelsen's own words, show how EPT addresses his specific concerns
**Goal**: This isn't just "biological speculation"—it resolves Kelsen's paradox
**Avoid**: Drifting from Kelsen to general evolutionary ethics (stay focused)

### Section 7 Strategy: Demonstrate Practical Payoff
**Technique**: Show how EPT explains puzzles in contemporary legal theory
**Goal**: Not just historical interest—active implications for current debates
**Avoid**: Over-claiming (we don't solve ALL legal theory problems)

### Section 8 Strategy: Preemptive Defense
**Technique**: Anticipate objections, state them more forcefully than critics would
**Goal**: Show we've thought through implications, not naive about challenges
**Avoid**: Defensive tone (confidence that EPT survives scrutiny)

### Conclusion Strategy: Invitation to Research Program
**Technique**: Frame as beginning of new agenda, not final word
**Goal**: Invite collaboration, not declare victory
**Avoid**: Triumphalist tone (Kelsen scholars won't be convinced by arrogance)
